Functions of Speech in English Grammar

1. Subject

The subject is the person, place, thing, or idea that performs the action of the verb or is described by it. In English sentences, the subject typically comes before the verb.

Example: "The cat sleeps on the mat."

Explanation: "The cat" is the subject because it is performing the action of sleeping.

2. Direct Object

The direct object is the noun or pronoun that receives the action of a transitive verb directly. It answers the question "What?" or "Whom?" in relation to the verb.

Example: "She read the book."

Explanation: "The book" is the direct object because it receives the action of reading.

3. Object of Preposition

The object of the preposition is the noun or pronoun that follows a preposition, creating a prepositional phrase that typically shows location, direction, time, or manner.

Example: "The cat sleeps on the mat."

Explanation: "The mat" is the object of the preposition "on."

4. Predicative Complement

The predicative complement (also called a subject complement) is a word or phrase that follows a linking verb (such as "be," "seem," "become") and provides more information about the subject or object.

Example: "She is a doctor."

Explanation: "A doctor" describes "she" and completes the meaning of the subject via the linking verb "is."

5. Modifier

A modifier is a word or phrase (often an adjective or adverb) that provides additional information about another word, such as a noun or verb.

Example: "She bought a beautiful dress."

Explanation: "Beautiful" modifies "dress" by describing its appearance.

6. Adjunct

An adjunct is a word or phrase that adds extra information to a sentence but is not essential to its core meaning. Adjuncts often indicate time, place, manner, frequency, or reason.

Example: "She went to the store yesterday."

Explanation: "Yesterday" is an adjunct of time, adding information about when she went but not changing the fundamental meaning of the sentence.

7. Determiner

A determiner is a word that comes before a noun to specify reference. Determiners can indicate things like definiteness (e.g., "the," "a"), quantity (e.g., "many," "few"), possession (e.g., "my," "his"), or other details.

Example: "The cat sleeps on the mat."

Explanation: "The" specifies the particular "cat" being referred to.
